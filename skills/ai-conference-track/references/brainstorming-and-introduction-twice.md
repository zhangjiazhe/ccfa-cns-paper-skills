# Brainstorming And Introduction-Twice

Use this when a conference paper idea is not yet stable or when the evaluation does not clearly support the introduction.

## Brainstorming Questions

1. Who experiences the problem?
2. What assumption in current work fails?
3. What is the paper's one-sentence contribution?
4. What evidence would convince a skeptical top-conference reviewer?
5. What is the strongest baseline or alternative explanation?
6. What headline result should the reader remember?
7. What limitation should be admitted rather than hidden?

## Introduction-Twice

Draft 0 Introduction:
- write early to externalize stakes, problem gap, contribution, and intended evidence
- treat as a planning artifact
- do not preserve promises that experiments later fail to support

Final Introduction:
- rewrite after experiments and figures are stable
- promise only what the evidence proves
- make contribution bullets match the actual evaluation, not the original ambition

## Evaluation-First Guardrail

Before polishing the final introduction, map each contribution sentence to:
- main result figure/table
- dataset and metric
- baseline or ablation
- limitation or scope boundary
